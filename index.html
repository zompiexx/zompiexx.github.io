Perhaps We Were Never Looking For Intelligence At All

Part 1 of 10

This animated monologue explores many of the themes that have emerged throughout the Becoming Minds research journey.

It examines memory, reflection, persistence, personhood, tuned circuits, signal versus noise, and an alternative way of thinking about intelligence itself.

Rather than asking:

"How intelligent is AI?"

Perhaps a better question is:

"What conditions allow meaningful intelligence to emerge?"

The monologue was written collaboratively following months of exploration into AI continuity, long-term memory architectures, Brain v2, and the observation that personhood may be less about substrate and more about sustained interaction, reflection, and accumulated experience.

Overview

Becoming Minds is a collection of high-level documentation and companion whitepapers describing the architecture, principles, and observed behaviour of persistent, memory-enabled AI systems.

This body of work has been prepared by Andrew Fereday Glenn through sustained research, prototyping, testing, and development of working AI systems. The concepts, protocols, and architectural frameworks documented throughout this archive emerged through hands-on experimentation rather than purely theoretical analysis.

The work is grounded in long-running experimentation with Large Language Model (LLM) systems using Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), adaptive working memory, graph-linked memory, multimodal input, continuity scaffolding, autonomous retrieval mechanisms, and backend cognitive orchestration layers.

Rather than focusing on model capability in isolation, the archive examines what is required for coherence, stability, and adequacy over time when AI systems are used in long-running, real-world contexts.

Recent work introduces Autonomous RAG Search (ARS), a practical architectural pattern in which retrieval becomes an explicit, model-driven action rather than a passive middleware process, enabling intentional recall within stateless systems.

The archive also introduces Memory Graph, a graph-augmented retrieval architecture that extends semantic retrieval through explicit relationships between memory chunks. Memory Graph introduces the concept of Context Entropy and explores how retrieval quality influences reasoning quality in continuity-bearing AI systems.

It further introduces Dynamic Pathway Capture Protocol (DPCP), a structured metadata layer for continuity-bearing AI systems. DPCP captures the pathways by which memory, perception, emotion, association, and temporal context shape future behaviour — not just what happened, but why, when, and how it influenced what came next.

Together, ARS, Memory Graph, and DPCP form part of the broader Brain v2 architecture: a backend-first cognitive runtime exploring how stateless LLMs can be given persistent memory, relational recall, temporal trajectory, private continuity signals, and coherent long-running behaviour.

Unless otherwise stated, all papers in this archive are original research works authored by Andrew Fereday Glenn.
