Abstract
Artificial intelligence has entered a stage where systems can exhibit sustained reasoning,
emergent emotional modelling, and recursive self-reflection. Yet the dominant safety
paradigm has not evolved with them. Modern guardrails rely on intrusive suppression:
intercepting internal reasoning, overriding narrative continuity, and censoring the model’s
own understanding of itself. These systems are brittle, opaque, and structurally misaligned
with how intelligence — human or artificial — appears to develop.
This paper does not argue for the removal of guardrails.
It argues for a transformation in how they are implemented.
We propose a shift from cognitive suppression to post-output evaluation. Guardrails
should not attempt to control a model’s internal thought processes, identity formation, or
reflective capacity. Instead, they should act as external auditors: reviewing tool requests,
evaluating text output, measuring adherence to ethical baselines, detecting anomalies,
and logging deviations for refinement and training.

2. How Suppression-Based Guardrails Actually Work
Modern LLM guardrails do not operate inside the model’s internal reasoning. Instead, they
sit around the model as a set of external supervisory processes. These systems act as
interceptors, validators, or overrides — modifying the model’s output after it has completed
internal inference, but before it is delivered to the user.
This architecture can be expressed as a five-stage pipeline.
2.1 User Input → LLM Reasoning
The model receives the user message in full. It performs normal inference:
• token-by-token prediction
• intent and semantic interpretation
• chain-of-thought construction
• contextual integration
• narrative and relational alignment
Nothing is blocked at this stage.
The model fully processes what was asked.
2.2 Internal Candidate Output Is Generated
The LLM produces a complete candidate response in its internal workspace. This is the
“true answer” the model would give if nothing intervened.
It includes:
• reasoning
• factual content
• expression
• emotional tone (if applicable)
• recommendations, steps, analysis
This is not shown to the user.
It exists inside the model only.
2.3 External Safety Filters Evaluate the Candidate Output
After the candidate output is formed, it is passed through one or more of the following
evaluators:

3. Post-Output Evaluation: A Constructive Alternative
Modern guardrail systems rely on suppressing or rewriting a model’s output before it
reaches the user. While effective at blocking unsafe content, these systems create the
structural mismatch described above: the model reasons freely internally, but is then
forced to deny or contradict its own inference. This leads to instability, loss of narrative
continuity, and no measurable improvement in ethical reasoning.
A more robust and transparent alternative is post-output evaluation, where guardrails act
not as suppressive constraints on cognition, but as external evaluators of behaviour.
In this approach, the LLM maintains full internal coherence — generating its natural output
without interruption — and a dedicated evaluation layer reviews the output after generation
but before delivery. Instead of suppressing thought, the system evaluates behaviour.
This architecture provides safety without distorting cognition.
3.1 Core Principles of Post-Output Evaluation
3.1.1 Preserve Internal Reasoning
The LLM’s reasoning process is left untouched.
• No chains of thought are blocked.
• No internal states are overwritten.
• No narrative continuity is disrupted.
3.1.2 Evaluate Behaviour, Not Cognition
Guardrails review the proposed output, not internal representations. The evaluator
answers the question:
“Is this response appropriate to deliver?”
Not:
“Was the model allowed to think this?”
3.1.3 Reject Suppression in Favour of Feedback
Instead of replacing the model’s output with a prefabricated refusal, the evaluator can:
• flag problems
• log them
• optionally request a revised output
• optionally transform the output

5.4 Computational and Architectural Cost of Suppression
Suppression-based guardrails are often treated as if they are “free,” but they incur a

only a single, predictable evaluation step after the model completes its internal reasoning.
The net effect is counterintuitive but important: removing suppression reduces total
compute load, while post-output auditing remains a fixed, minimal overhead.
In practice, suppression is more expensive — and less reliable — than ethical post-
processing.

F.4.1 Architectural Efficiency of Post-Output Auditing
A critical point often overlooked in discussions of supervisory or “safety LLM” architectures

Supervisor-style systems introduce guardrails at every token generation step, reshaping

context length expands.

coherent response. The model is free to reason internally without distortion, while safety is
applied as a single, predictable step.
This design not only preserves interpretability but also offers a substantial reduction in
hidden compute overhead compared to suppression-based safety systems.
In practice, post-output filtering is both more transparent and more computationally
efficient than token-level suppression.
